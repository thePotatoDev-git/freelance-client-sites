72 Hours in Berlin

As much as we all would love to stay in one city for a week while traveling, sometimes you find yourself in a situation where you want to travel to multiple countries and multiple cities, only allowing yourself 2-3 days in each. This is typically the case the past few times I’ve traveled to Europe, so with that said here is a travel guide for those looking to explore Berlin in 72 hours.

Here are the major things this guide will help you with

  • How to Get to Berlin
  • Getting Around Berlin
  • Where to Stay
  • Sites to Visit (72hr Itinerary)
  • Where to Eat

How to Get to Berlin

Since Berlin is a cosmopolitan city it is easily accessible from all types of transportation such as ground, train, and airplane. While ground and train transportation can vary depending on your origin, I can let you know that if you do fly into Berlin you’ll be landing at Brandenberg Airport “Willy Brandt”.

From the airport you’ll have easy access to the D-Bahn and S-Bahn trains that can take you right into the city. Again, depending on where you stay one option might be better than the other, however, the main airport express line known as the Flughafen Express (FEX) is the quickest and cheapest way into the city center and the central station: Berlin Hauptbahnhof (HBF). The train ride is €3.80 and takes about 30 minutes.

Experience Tip 1: Find the ticket machines with a DB on it and don’t forget to validate your ticket when you get to the platform.

Experience Tip 2: If you are taking the FEX back to Brandenburg Airport, make sure you search for “Flughafen” instead of “airport” or “Brandenburg”. You can buy tickets on the platform, just make sure you check the digital timetable to see which platform the train leaves from.

Getting Around Berlin

The nice thing about Berlin is that there are many ways to explore the city, with easy access to restaurants, shopping, and all the sites that the city has to offer.

  • Walking: You can walk to most of the major sites in Berlin if staying in Mitte. The Brandenburg Gate and the Reichstag Building are about a 20 minute walk, Berliner Dom is a 30 minute walk, and you’ll run into many restaurants and cafes along the way. If you end up walking around so much, you can always hop on a train to get back to where you need to go since the U-Bahn and S-Bahn are accessible throughout the city.
  • Biking: Many locals and tourists alike can be seen biking throughout the city. The roads are bike friendly making it a quick and safe way to get to each site you want to visit.
  • Electric Scooters: This was a little surprising to see in Europe, but I feel like I saw more e-scooters than bikes! Just as bikes, they are a speedier way than walking to explore the city.
  • Train: There are literally U-Bahn stations in every major part of the city center making this mode of transportation very easy and very desirable. If you do need to travel a little further than where you’re staying the S-Bahn might be quicker, but either line is quick way to see the city.

Experience Tip 3: You can buy any U-Bahn or S-Bahn ticket at a station platform or use an app to manage your tickets. You’ll need to validate your physical ticket at a machine. The times I’ve used both the S and U-Bahns we never got our tickets checked, however, if they do check and you don’t have a valid ticket you’ll be fined up to double the ticket price.

Where to Stay

There will be many factors to decide where to stay in Berlin, but if you do want to be in a central area that is walking distance to most of the sites, Berlin Central, S-Bahn, and the U-Bahn - I would recommend staying in the area of Mitte. There are many lodging options in the area and a variety of price points suitable for every kind of traveler.

Sites to Visit

Below I’ll map out an ideal 72hr itinerary to try and see as much as possible in Berlin.

Day 1

Start your day by heading to Brandenburg Gate. From the gate there are multiple routes you can take since many landmarks are around that general vicinity. If you make an immediate left after passing under the gate to the main road, you’ll come across the Memorial to the Murdered Jews of Europe. Here you can walk through the stones above and then make your way around to the museum entrance that runs just underneath. Admission is free.

Next, walk back towards the gate but cross the street to walk through the Tiergarten and make your way towards the Victory Column. The walk to the column is just under a mile from the gate and once you make it to the round-a-bout look for underground stairs on your left or right of the road to reach the base of the column in the middle. Don’t let your almost-one-mile walk go to waste! Head into the column to get a ticket for €4.00 to allow you access to the top. One your way to the top you’ll get to walk through a “famous landmark” exhibition and then get some fresh air at the bottom lookout terrace. After some fresh air, head back in to tackle the never-ending spiral staircase that takes you to the top.

Experience Tip 4: Make sure to have good walking shoes today and do some light stretching before and after you tackle the staircase! Don’t be alarmed after the climb back down when your quads start to tighten up a little bit.

The 360 degree view at the top of the column is worth it. You get a bird’s eye view of the beautiful city of Berlin and if you happen to be there during fall, the mix of autumn colors in the leaves is breathtaking. After taking in the amazing views, head down and make your way back towards the gate. Veer right through the Tiergarten and you’ll run into Potsdamer Platz. This area is a good place to stop for a lunch break and stroll through the area to see the Historic Traffic Light, the Berliner Mauer, or the Spy Museum.

Again, head back towards the gate, but this time walk straight by it to run into the Reichstag Building. Making your way to the top of the building also guarantees you amazing views of the city to take in.
